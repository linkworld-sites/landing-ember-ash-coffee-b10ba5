There's a version of this story that starts with a pivotal moment — a cup of coffee in a back-street café somewhere photogenic, a revelation, a calling. That's not this story.

This story starts with a spreadsheet, and a lot of questions we couldn't answer yet.

## Why roast to order?

The economics of coffee roasting push you toward efficiency. Roast large, ship fast, replenish warehouses on a schedule. The beans that have been sitting for six weeks taste like six-week-old beans. Most people don't know this, because most coffee is sold without a roast date — and when there's no date, there's nothing to compare against.

We decided early that we'd print the roast date on every bag. That one commitment shapes everything downstream. It means we can't pre-roast. It means we don't have inventory sitting on shelves. It means we roast in small batches, on demand, and ship within 24 hours.

It's more work. It's the right way to do it.

## The drum

We run a 12-kilogram drum roaster — small enough that every batch is supervised by hand, large enough to produce consistent results. The drum heats evenly, which matters more than most people realize. Uneven heat means uneven development: some beans finish before others, and the bag tastes chaotic.

Our roast profiles took three months to develop. We cup every lot before it ships. If it doesn't taste like what's written on the bag, we adjust the profile and try again. There's no shortcut here.

## The first lot: Ethiopia Guji Natural

The Ethiopia Guji Natural was the first coffee we were confident enough to put on sale. The lot comes from a single washing station in the Shakiso district — smallholder farmers delivering cherry the morning after picking, dried naturally on raised beds for 21 to 28 days.

The result is dense and tropical: stone fruit, jasmine, and a finish that stays warm. It tastes like somewhere specific. That's what single-origin means to us — not a marketing claim, but a flavour that could only come from that place, in that harvest, from those trees.

> Stone fruit, jasmine, and a long, warm finish. That's the Guji.

## What comes next

We have two more lots arriving: a Washed Guatemala Huehuetenango that's all dark chocolate and walnut, and a Honey Process Colombia Nariño with red plum and caramel. Both are good. We'll tell you when they're ready.

We'll also tell you about what didn't work — a Kenyan lot that we turned down because the acidity felt harsh rather than bright, and a Sumatra that we couldn't get to behave on our profile. Not every coffee belongs in every roaster's hands. Knowing when to say no is part of the craft.

We write when there's something worth saying. This was worth saying.
